Let’s start with the Russia-Ukraine conflict a Russian blogger with a knack for dramatics, paints a vivid picture of chaotic skies, noting that Ukraine has edged ahead in the drone warfare game. He claims that the sky is filling up with first-person view (FPV) drones, making it extremely difficult to do target differentiation. It does bring up a very interesting point that as drone swarms start increasing in size, there does appear to need to be a red v blue target identification system that needs to be created, and at very very low cost. Of course, it would also have to be very secure from interception as well, with some sort of crypto. It also points to the fact that these larger swarms are going to need target coordination as well, so they don’t hit the same target more than once unnecessarily, etc.

Ukrainian drones have reportedly been armed with some sort of recoilless grenade launcher. Who needs crewed close air support when drones can rain down destruction with precision? “strafing infantry targets with grenade launchers”

On the technological front, the Russians have also entered the drone arms race with a new contender—the Molot-Britva CUAS drone interceptor. This drone is reportedly capable of intercepting other drones, flaunting speeds of 200-350 km/h and hitting targets up to 3.5 km away. Similarly, a clip showed the Russian Yolka UAV interceptor, which showed the full interception from beginning to end, from a ground-based human asset to direct interception. Both of these are good enough and relatively compact enough that I wouldn’t be surprised if this actually made a pretty good dent in the Ukrainian offensive capability if they become widespread. It is one thing to have general recon, but it is quite another for troops to be able to protect themselves when they hear a drone nearby. That said, this probably won’t work so hot for low-flying FPV drones with lots of confusing background information for the sensors.

“Russian Yolka UAV interceptor”

Meanwhile, Ukraine’s tech whizzes aren’t sitting idle either. They’ve unveiled the SlimBeam laser turret from Fulltime Robotics, a potentially useful tool for destroying drones. It has a 1.5 kW output and is capable of blinding pesky adversaries up to 2 km away. The problem obviously is that these things take a meaningful amount of time to start burning a hole in the adversary equipment. The problem is that these things move. So I think the real intention is to hit the sensors, to destroy the most sensitive parts of the drone. Part of me is thinking that they should make the beams far wider rather than overly narrow. Yes, that takes way way more power, but the advantage is you are much more likely to hit the sensitive parts of the drone, like a laser shotgun vs a laser sniper rifle.
